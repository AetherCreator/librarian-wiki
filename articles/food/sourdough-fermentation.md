# Sourdough Fermentation

## Summary
Sourdough fermentation is a complex process where wild yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae and related species) and lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus genus) metabolize flour sugars through anaerobic and aerobic pathways, producing CO2 for leavening and lactic/acetic acids that give sourdough its characteristic flavor and extended shelf life.

## Core Knowledge
### Microbial Ecology

Sourdough fermentation is a controlled microbial ecosystem where microorganisms interact and adapt to their environment. The dominant species in a sourdough starter are Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis (now reclassified as Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis) and wild Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast, which form a stable symbiotic relationship. This balance is crucial for the fermentation process, as it determines the flavor profile and leavening capacity of the bread.

### Metabolic Niche Theory

The metabolic division between LAB and wild yeast is key to understanding the stability of the symbiosis. Amylase enzymes in flour convert starch to simple sugars (maltose and glucose), which are preferentially consumed by LAB, leaving maltose for wild yeast. This niche partitioning at the carbohydrate level explains why the yeast and LAB coexist rather than competing.

### Phytase Activity

LAB phytase activity reduces phytic acid (an antinutrient in grain bran) by up to 90% via phytase enzymes produced by LAB. This significantly increases mineral bioavailability (iron, zinc, magnesium), making sourdough a more nutritious option.
